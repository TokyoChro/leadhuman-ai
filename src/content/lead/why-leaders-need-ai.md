I keep hearing the same thing from senior leaders. "AI is important, sure. But I'm a people leader. The technical stuff is what my IT team is for."

I get the instinct. Leadership has always been about people. The best leaders I've worked with in Tokyo build trust, create psychological safety, develop talent, and make hard calls under uncertainty. None of that requires a ChatGPT subscription.

But here's what I keep seeing on the ground working with leaders across industries. The ones who dismiss AI as 'not my department' are already falling behind. Not because AI is replacing what they do. Because AI is reshaping the environment they do it in.

And if you don't understand the environment you can't lead in it.

---

## The Delegation Gap

Most leaders haven't answered the three questions that matter most right now.

What can I delegate to AI? Not in theory. In practice. In their actual workflow with their actual team this quarter. A training needs analysis that used to take three weeks can now take three days. That changes your planning timeline. That changes what you promise stakeholders.

What should I protect from AI? This is the question most people skip. Feedback conversations. Relationship building. Creative ideation that requires lived experience. These get worse when you outsource them. Knowing that boundary is a leadership skill now.

How is AI changing my team's expectations? Your direct reports are already using AI whether you've sanctioned it or not. Some are using it well. Some are producing mediocre work faster. A leader who understands the tool can tell the difference and coach accordingly.

[Chowdhury et al. (2023)](https://consensus.app/papers/artificial-intelligence-adoption-in-a-professional-yang-blount/a9b6dec2c5a352079bc9b30e4f5cb39c/) studied AI adoption across professional service firms and found that top management support is consistently the strongest driver of organizational AI adoption. Not the IT budget. Not the tools. The leaders.

---

> **The biggest barrier to AI adoption in most organizations isn't technical. It's cultural. And culture starts with the leader.**

---

## Why 'I'm Not Technical' Is the Wrong Frame

I'm not talking about becoming a power user. I'm talking about a baseline that changes how you make decisions.

[Transformational leadership research](https://consensus.app/papers/understanding-user-attitudes-towards-aienabled-shao-makady/15cc77dfd8c258d5b53581a9f8320c7a/) consistently shows that when leaders visibly engage with new technologies, employees' self efficacy and willingness to experiment goes up. It's not about the leader's skill level. It's about what the leader's behavior signals.

When a senior leader says "I'm too old for this" they think they're being humble. What they're actually doing is giving everyone else permission to opt out. And in an organization where the top doesn't experiment, the middle won't either.

The frame isn't "am I technical enough." The frame is "am I curious enough to model the behavior I need from my team."

---

## Three Things to Try This Week

> **1. Spend one hour experimenting.** Pick one thing you do every week that involves synthesizing information. Reading reports. Preparing for meetings. Summarizing feedback. Try using AI to help. Notice what it does well and where it falls short. The goal isn't to become proficient. It's to build a felt sense of what AI can and can't do.

> **2. Have one honest conversation with your team.** Ask them: "Are you using AI? What for? What's working?" I promise you the answers will surprise you. Some people on your team are already 10x more productive with AI than you realize. Others are struggling quietly. You won't know until you ask.

> **3. Get clear on your one boundary.** What is the one part of your leadership that should never be automated? For me it's feedback conversations. For you it might be something different. Naming that boundary makes you more confident about using AI for everything else.

---

The leaders who thrive in the next decade won't be the most technical. They'll be the ones who are curious enough to understand the tools reshaping their environment and wise enough to know where the human work still lives.

That combination of technological fluency and human depth? That's the leadership competency nobody is hiring for yet. But they will be.

What's stopping you from experimenting? Genuinely curious whether it's time, skepticism, or something else entirely.
