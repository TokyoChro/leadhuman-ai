## Article Draft

Something I've noticed watching companies roll out AI tools over the past year: the leaders who are most frustrated with the results are almost always the same leaders who skipped the training step entirely.

Not because they're bad leaders but because they assumed the tools were intuitive enough for people to figure out on their own.

The thing about AI adoption is that tool access and skill development are not the same thing and organizations keep treating them as if they are. A Boston Consulting Group study of nearly 1,500 workers found that productivity actually decreased when employees used four or more AI tools. The more tools mandated, the worse the output. That's the kind of finding that should stop a leadership meeting cold, and it rarely does.

Researchers are calling the resulting condition 'AI brain fry' and the name is almost too accurate. Workers using multiple AI tools describe a persistent mental fog, slowed decision making, and a kind of cognitive buildup that accumulates across the day. An eight month UC Berkeley study of a 200 person tech firm found that AI tools increased employee workload rather than reducing it and acted as a net drag on efficiency. The tools were doing something. Just not what anyone expected.

The irony is that 60% of organizations are still in the experimental phase with AI and haven't moved to full integration, but they're already asking their people to use it daily. And only one in four workers is receiving any formal AI training from their employer. That's not a training budget issue. That's a design issue.

> When a tool multiplies the volume of work faster than a person can evaluate the output, you don't have an efficiency gain. You have a new kind of bottleneck.

Leaders tend to think of AI rollouts as technology decisions and L&D teams tend to think of them as skills problems and neither group is quite talking to the other. I've seen this gap up close in companies across different industries and it's almost always the same story. The AI strategy meeting happens without an L&D person in the room and the L&D team hears about the tools months later and tries to build training retroactively for something that's already embedded in daily workflows.

There's also the 'workslop' problem, which is a term now circulating in workplace research to describe the flood of AI generated content that looks polished on the surface but requires significant human cleanup. 66% of workers report spending six or more hours every week fixing AI errors. That is a full workday of invisible rework that no productivity dashboard is measuring.

Here's what I think actually helps:

> 1. Audit before you add. Before the next AI tool gets rolled out, ask how many tools your team is already using and how confident they feel using them. If the answer is "not sure", that's your starting point and probably your whole conversation for that week.

> 2. Bring L&D into the AI conversation early. Not after rollout and not for a one time training session but as a design partner from the beginning, because the people who understand how adults learn under pressure are exactly who should be shaping AI adoption strategy.

> 3. Measure rework, not just speed. If your team is faster but spending hours correcting AI output, you haven't gained productivity. Build visibility into what the cleanup actually costs before the next quarterly review lands on your desk.

> 4. Normalize not knowing. The 'AI brain fry' research suggests that cognitive overload is partly driven by the pressure to look competent with tools people haven't been properly trained on. A team that feels safe saying "I don't know how to use this well yet" is going to learn faster than one that's quietly drowning.

To be frank, the organizations that will get the most out of AI in the next few years aren't the ones with the most tools or the most aggressive rollout timelines. They're the ones where someone stopped and asked "do our people actually know how to use this?"

That question sounds simple. It's apparently quite rare.
